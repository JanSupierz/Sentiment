\section{Sentiment Analysis}

Sentiment analysis (or opinion mining) is an umbrella term for the computational study of people's opinions, sentiments, emotions, and attitudes expressed in written language \cite{Liu2012sentiment}. The term includes field names and slightly different tasks associated with them: sentiment analysis, opinion mining, opinion extraction, sentiment mining, subjectivity analysis, affect analysis, emotion analysis, review mining, etc. \cite{Liu2012sentiment} However, the main terms in academia remain both: sentiment analysis and opinion mining \cite{Liu2012sentiment}.

A foundational formalization was proposed by \cite{Liu2012sentiment}, who recognized that opinions are not monolithic but rather composed of multiple interacting elements. This insight led to defining an opinion as a quintuple:
\[
(e_i, a_{ij}, s_{ijkl}, h_k, t_l)
\]
where:
\begin{itemize}
	\item $e_i$ is the name of an entity (e.g., a product, service, organization, or individual),
	\item $a_{ij}$ is an aspect of $e_i$ (a specific feature or attribute of that entity),
	\item $s_{ijkl}$ is the sentiment expressed toward aspect $a_{ij}$ of entity $e_i$,
	\item $h_k$ is the opinion holder (the person or organization expressing the opinion), and
	\item $t_l$ is the time when the opinion is expressed by $h_k$.
\end{itemize}

Following this definition, the main objective of sentiment analysis is to determine all quintuples present in given texts \cite{azzeddine2018sentiment}.

\subsection{Sentiment Analysis Levels}

Sentiment analysis tasks are typically categorized by their granularity of analysis, reflecting different levels at which opinions can be extracted and aggregated \cite{azzeddine2018sentiment}. The choice of granularity determines both the complexity of the task and the type of insights that can be obtained. Three main levels of analysis are recognized in the literature:

\begin{itemize}	
	\item \textbf{Document-level sentiment analysis (DLSA)} operates at the coarsest granularity, aiming to determine the overall opinion expressed in an entire document \cite{behdenna2018document}. Following the opinion quintuple formulation, DLSA identifies the overall sentiment of the opinion holder about the entity \cite{azzeddine2018sentiment}. Documents are typically classified as positive, negative, or neutral \cite{sharma2025review}, under the assumption that the document focuses on a single entity or topic, and that the opinion is coherent \cite{sharma2025review}. The task commonly follows a two-step approach: \textit{Topic Relevance Retrieval} followed by \textit{Opinion Finding} \cite{azzeddine2018sentiment, missen2012opinion}. Considered the simplest sentiment analysis task, DLSA has been the most frequently studied granularity in the field, with 73 out of 159 reviewed articles operating at this level \cite{azzeddine2018sentiment, ravi2015survey}.
	
	\item \textbf{Sentence-level sentiment analysis (SLSA)} operates at a finer granularity by considering individual sentences as the unit of analysis \cite{behdenna2018document}. This level typically involves a two-step process. The first step, known as \textit{Subjectivity Classification}, distinguishes subjective sentences (expressing opinions) from objective sentences (presenting facts) \cite{azzeddine2018sentiment, CHATURVEDI201865}. The second step, \textit{Sentence Sentiment Classification}, then assigns polarity labels (positive, negative, or neutral) to subjective sentences \cite{azzeddine2018sentiment}. This approach is particularly useful when documents contain a mixture of factual statements and opinions \cite{sharma2025review}. However, a known limitation is that objective sentences can sometimes carry implicit sentiment \cite{behdenna2018document}. Furthermore, sentence-level analysis commonly assumes that each sentence expresses a single sentiment; sentences containing multiple opposing sentiments require more nuanced treatment \cite{azzeddine2018sentiment}.
	
	\item \textbf{Aspect-based sentiment analysis (ABSA)} represents the finest granularity, focusing on identifying sentiments expressed towards specific aspects or attributes of an entity \cite{sharma2025review}. Rather than assigning a single polarity to an entire document or sentence, ABSA extracts both the target of opinion and its associated sentiment, corresponding to the first three components of the opinion quintuple: entity, aspect, and sentiment \cite{behdenna2018document, azzeddine2018sentiment}. For example, in a restaurant review, the sentiment expressed towards ``food'' may differ from that towards ``service'' \cite{sharma2025review}. This fine-grained analysis addresses the need to know exactly what people liked or disliked \cite{azzeddine2018sentiment}. Aspects may be expressed either explicitly or implicitly \cite{azzeddine2018sentiment}, and ABSA consequently requires two core subtasks: \textit{Aspect Extraction} and \textit{Aspect-Level Sentiment Classification} \cite{sharma2025review}.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Sentiment Analysis Tasks}

The explosive growth of user-generated content on social media, review platforms, and forums has made automated sentiment detection an essential tool for businesses, governments, and researchers \cite{sharma2025review}. Applications span brand monitoring, customer feedback analysis, political trend prediction, and mental health assessment \cite{sharma2025review}. More broadly, the field supports business intelligence, recommendation systems, review summarization, and government intelligence, demonstrating its impact \cite{behdenna2018document}. As recent comprehensive surveys discuss \cite{sharma2025review}, the field has evolved beyond simple polarity detection to address multiple complementary problems. Below are some of the recent fields of research:

\begin{itemize}	
	\item \textbf{Emotion detection (ED)} extends polarity classification by identifying fine-grained emotional states such as joy, anger, sadness, or frustration \cite{sharma2025review}. Unlike binary or ternary sentiment classification, emotion detection requires more expressive representations and often relies on specialized lexical resources or machine learning algorithms \cite{sharma2025review}.
	
	\item \textbf{Multi-domain sentiment classification} addresses the challenge of transferring models across different application domains \cite{sharma2025review}. For instance, a model trained on movie reviews may perform poorly on product reviews due to differences in vocabulary and linguistic patterns. Domain adaptation and transfer learning techniques are commonly employed to bridge this gap \cite{sharma2025review}.
	
	\item \textbf{Multilingual sentiment analysis} aims to analyse sentiment across multiple languages \cite{sharma2025review}. This task typically leverages language-specific resources such as sentiment lexicons, large translated corpora, or language detection systems as preprocessing steps \cite{sharma2025review}.
	
	\item \textbf{Multimodal sentiment analysis (MMSA)} integrates information from multiple channels, including text, audio, and visual signals \cite{sharma2025review}. By moving beyond purely textual analysis, MMSA enables more advanced applications such as analysing spoken reviews and vlogs, interpreting images and social media tags, and understanding human–machine interactions \cite{sharma2025review}.
	
	\item \textbf{Opinion spam detection (OSD)} focuses on identifying deceptive or fabricated opinions intended to mislead users or automated sentiment analysis tools \cite{sharma2025review}. These deceptive opinions include fake reviews designed to manipulate public perception and social media posts that artificially promote specific individuals, products, or services \cite{sharma2025review}.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Sentiment Analysis Challenges}

Despite its importance and wide-ranging applications, sentiment analysis faces several fundamental challenges that complicate accurate opinion mining. Opinion words are necessary but not sufficient for reliable sentiment detection, and multiple linguistic and contextual factors must be addressed \cite{behdenna2018document}.

\begin{itemize}	
	\item \textbf{Contextual Polarity Ambiguity} is the core difficulty: the same sentiment-bearing word can have different polarities depending on the context in which it appears \cite{behdenna2018document}. A word that is positive in one domain may be neutral or even negative in another, requiring models to capture subtle contextual signals \cite{sharma2025review}.
	
	\item \textbf{Sarcasm and Irony Detection} present particularly difficult challenges, as the literal meaning of an utterance contradicts its intended sentiment \cite{behdenna2018document, sharma2025review}. When someone says something positive but actually means something negative, or vice versa, purely lexical approaches inevitably fail \cite{behdenna2018document}. A significant portion of recent research has emphasized the need for improved models capable of classifying sarcasm and irony \cite{sharma2025review}.
	
	\item \textbf{Negation Handling} is critical because the presence of negation words can reverse the polarity of an opinion \cite{behdenna2018document}.
	
	\item \textbf{Implicit and Objective Opinions} are not always straightforward. Many sentences containing opinion words may not actually express any opinion, while conversely, many sentences without explicit opinion words can convey strong sentiments \cite{behdenna2018document}. These are often objective sentences that present factual information yet carry implicit evaluative meaning \cite{behdenna2018document}.
	
	\item \textbf{Vocabulary Challenges} include rapid vocabulary evolution, including new words, slang, and abbreviations that constantly emerge-particularly on social media \cite{sharma2025review}. Any practical system must therefore be robust to such linguistic shifts \cite{sharma2025review}. Broader challenges identified in the literature include algorithm optimization, language model limitations, subjectivity detection, tone-setting polarities, and data inconsistency \cite{sharma2025review}. Multilingual analysis adds further complexity, requiring either language-specific resources or large translated corpora \cite{sharma2025review}.
	
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Sentiment Analysis Methods}

Sentiment analysis methods can be broadly divided into two main categories: traditional approaches and machine learning approaches \cite{sharma2025review}. Within machine learning, a further distinction exists between classic supervised approaches and modern deep learning techniques \cite{behdenna2018document}. A detailed explanation of each method can be found in the following sections.

\section{Lexicon-Based Methods}

Lexicon-based approaches represent a fundamental alternative to supervised machine learning, operating without requiring labelled training data \cite{azzeddine2018sentiment}. These methods rely on pre-compiled dictionaries of words annotated with sentiment scores (e.g., positive, negative, neutral) and are often considered a form of unsupervised sentiment analysis \cite{azzeddine2018sentiment}. The core assumption is that the collective polarity of a sentence or document can be derived from the polarities of its individual words and phrases \cite{azzeddine2018sentiment}.

\subsection{Dictionary-Based versus Corpus-Based Approaches}

Lexicon-based methods are divided into two main categories: dictionary-based and corpus-based approaches \cite{ravi2015survey,azzeddine2018sentiment, nursal2025battle}. 

\begin{itemize}	
\item \textbf{Dictionary-based approaches} utilise existing lexical databases containing opinion words along with their sentiment strengths \cite{azzeddine2018sentiment}. Popular sentiment lexicons in this category include SentiWordNet \cite{sentiwordnet2010}, WordNet-Affect \cite{strapparava2004wordnet}, and VADER \cite{hutto2015vader} as stated in \cite{nursal2025battle}. The dictionary-based method is generally more practical as it does not require a comprehensive corpus covering all possible words \cite{nursal2025battle}.

\item \textbf{Corpus-based approaches}, by contrast, rely on the probability of sentiment words co-occurring with positive or negative seed words within large text collections \cite{azzeddine2018sentiment}. This method is particularly useful for discovering new sentiment words from domain-specific corpora or for creating entirely new sentiment lexicons \cite{nursal2025battle}. However, its effectiveness depends on the availability of a sufficiently large and representative corpus \cite{nursal2025battle}.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Semantic Orientation and PMI}

A pioneering unsupervised algorithm was proposed by Turney \cite{azzeddine2018sentiment, behdenna2018document}, who introduced a method based on semantic orientation (SO). The algorithm proceeds in three steps: first, extracting two consecutive words whose part-of-speech tags follow a specific patterns; second, estimating the polarity of adjectives and adverbs using pointwise mutual information (PMI); and third, aggregating individual polarities to classify the entire review \cite{azzeddine2018sentiment}.

PMI measures the statistical dependence between a phrase and reference words (typically ``excellent'' and ``poor'') based on their co-occurrence in a corpus or across the web \cite{azzeddine2018sentiment}. A sentence receives positive SO when it has stronger associations with ``excellent'' and negative SO when associated with ``poor'' \cite{behdenna2018document}. Rothfels and Tibshirani extended this approach by using two seed reference sets rather than single words, improving domain adaptability  \cite{behdenna2018document}.

\subsection{Sentiment Lexicons}

Tools such as \textsc{WordNet-Affect} and \textsc{SentiWordNet} provide foundational lexical resources for sentiment analysis \cite{nursal2025battle}.

\begin{itemize}	
\item \textbf{WordNet} organises nouns, verbs, adjectives, and adverbs into synsets (sets of cognitive synonyms), each representing a distinct concept \cite{nursal2025battle}. WordNet 3.0 contains over 117,000 synsets, with semantic relations connecting them \cite{nursal2025battle}. WordNet is an example of a well-known semantic network where definitional networks focus on IsA relationships between a concept and a newly defined sub-type, supporting the rule of inheritance for copying properties defined for a super-type to all its sub-types \cite{CHATURVEDI201865}.

\item \textbf{SentiWordNet} builds upon WordNet by assigning three scores to each synset: positivity (P), negativity (N), and objectivity (O), which sum to 1 \cite{nursal2025battle}. Version 3.0 covers 147,306 synsets and has been widely adopted due to its extensive coverage of opinion words \cite{nursal2025battle}. However, SentiWordNet is sensitive to noise in datasets, particularly from social media, and does not account for internet slang or emoticons \cite{nursal2025battle}. Lexicons generated with automatic methods often include neutral words, introducing noise in the detection of subjectivity \cite{CHATURVEDI201865}.

\item \textbf{VADER} (Valence Aware Dictionary and sEntiment Reasoner) \cite{hutto2015vader} is an effective rule-based tool, which combines a sentiment lexicon with grammatical and syntactical cues such as intensifiers, negations, and punctuation \cite{nursal2025battle}. VADER was specifically developed for social media contexts and incorporates acronyms (e.g., ``LOL''), slang, and emojis \cite{nursal2025battle}. The dictionary comprises approximately 7,500 features, drawing from existing lexicons including LIWC, ANEW, and the General Inquirer \cite{nursal2025battle}. VADER offers several advantages: it works effectively with social media text, requires no training data, supports emoji sentiment classification, and achieves high processing speed \cite{nursal2025battle}.

\item \textbf{TextBlob} provides another accessible lexicon-based tool, offering simple polarity scoring with preprocessing capabilities through the Natural Language Toolkit (NLTK) \cite{nursal2025battle}.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Empirical Comparisons}

Comparative studies have evaluated these lexicons across various domains. Nursal et al. \cite{nursal2025battle} compared WordNet, SentiWordNet, TextBlob, and VADER on Malaysian high-rise property reviews, using human-annotated data as a baseline. Their findings revealed that all four lexicons identified a higher proportion of positive reviews than negative or neutral ones. WordNet recorded the highest number of positive sentiments, followed closely by SentiWordNet and VADER \cite{nursal2025battle}. For negative sentiment detection, VADER proved most effective, while for neutral sentiment, VADER again detected the highest number of reviews \cite{nursal2025battle}.

In terms of classification accuracy against human annotation, VADER achieved the best performance with 78.2\% accuracy, 74.3\% precision, 90.9\% recall, and an F1-score of 81.7\% \cite{nursal2025battle}. TextBlob ranked second, followed by SentiWordNet and WordNet with comparable performance around 69–70\% accuracy \cite{nursal2025battle}.

However, misclassifications occurred due to several factors: limited vocabulary coverage, difficulty detecting sarcasm, word ambiguity, and challenges with misspelled or abbreviated terms \cite{nursal2025battle}.

\subsection{Handling Sentiment Shifters and Composition}

Effective lexicon-based systems must account for sentiment shifters—linguistic elements that alter the polarity of words \cite{azzeddine2018sentiment, behdenna2018document}. Negations (e.g., ``not good'') reverse semantic polarity, while intensifiers (e.g., ``very good'') modify the degree of sentiment \cite{azzeddine2018sentiment}. Sophisticated approaches incorporate three pillars: a sentiment lexicon containing words, phrases, idioms, and composition rules; a set of rules for handling shifters and contrastive clauses (e.g., ``but''); and sentiment aggregation functions derived from parse trees \cite{azzeddine2018sentiment}.

\subsection{Preprocessing Considerations}

Preprocessing requirements vary across lexicons. While VADER can handle capitalization, punctuation, and colloquial expressions with minimal preprocessing, tools such as TextBlob, SentiWordNet, and WordNet require rigorous cleaning \cite{nursal2025battle}. Typical preprocessing steps include tokenisation, lowercase conversion, spell-checking, stemming, stopword removal, part-of-speech tagging, lemmatisation, and noise removal \cite{nursal2025battle}. Normalisation of abbreviations and acronyms is particularly important for social media text, as inconsistent vocabulary can degrade accuracy \cite{nursal2025battle}.

\subsection{Advantages and Limitations}

The main advantage of lexicon-based methods is that they require minimal effort for manual data annotation and do not need training datasets, making them readily applicable across domains \cite{nursal2025battle}. They are particularly competitive when labelled data is scarce \cite{azzeddine2018sentiment}. However, these approaches are inherently limited to the words present in the lexicon and often cannot capture contextual nuances \cite{sharma2025review}. Domain dependency remains a challenge, as sentiment lexicons developed for one domain may perform poorly in another \cite{behdenna2018document}. Additionally, lexicon-based methods struggle with sarcasm, ambiguity, and the evolving nature of language, particularly on social media platforms \cite{nursal2025battle}.

\section{Classic Machine Learning Models}

With the availability of labelled datasets, supervised machine learning became the dominant paradigm for both coarse-grained and fine-grained sentiment analysis \cite{azzeddine2018sentiment}. Unlike lexicon-based methods, supervised approaches require annotated training data but can learn complex patterns from feature representations.

\subsection{Feature Engineering}

Generally text is first converted into a numerical representation, typically a \textit{bag-of-words} (BoW) model where each document is represented by a vector of word frequencies. Variants include \textit{n-grams} (contiguous sequences of words) and \textit{TF-IDF} (term frequency–inverse document frequency) weighting, which down-weights common words \cite{behdenna2018document}. 

Beyond these basic features, supervised approaches commonly employ syntactic features such as part-of-speech tags and dependency relations, as well as semantic features including sentiment words, phrases, and sentiment shifters \cite{azzeddine2018sentiment}. Feature engineering is the key to building effective sentiment analysis classifiers \cite{azzeddine2018sentiment}.

Sentence-level analysis is important because it permits a fine-grained view of different opinions expressed \cite{CHATURVEDI201865}. Sentence-level features can be in the form of n-grams, POS tags, or location based features \cite{CHATURVEDI201865}. Studies examining the different types of sentence features and their comparative effectiveness in determining sentiment analysis concluded that features such as length or rhetorical segments in text have little effect in accuracy and could sometimes decrease sentiment accuracy; similarly, bi-grams performed well without the need for POS features \cite{CHATURVEDI201865}.

\subsection{Classification Algorithms}

Classifiers such as Naïve Bayes (NB), Logistic Regression, and Support Vector Machines (SVM) are then trained on these feature vectors. SVM and NB, in particular, have been shown to be the most effective classifiers for sentiment analysis tasks \cite{behdenna2018document}. Extracted features are used to train state-of-the-art classifiers such as SVM and NB that assume that the class of a particular feature is independent of the class of other features given the training data \cite{CHATURVEDI201865}.

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Support Vector Machines (SVM)} perform well on high-dimensional sparse text data due to their margin-maximization objective and have been widely adopted for sentiment classification \cite{behdenna2018document}. 

\item \textbf{Naïve Bayes (NB)} offers simplicity and efficiency, often achieving competitive results despite its strong independence assumptions \cite{behdenna2018document}. 
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Empirical Findings}

\begin{itemize}
\item Pang was the first to apply machine learning to movie review classification, showing that binary feature representation outperforms frequency-based representations \cite{behdenna2018document}. 

\item Pak and Paroubek proposed sub-graph representations from syntactic dependency trees, showing that such structured features avoid information loss associated with pure n-gram models and improve SVM performance on French reviews \cite{behdenna2018document}.

\item For Arabic sentiment analysis, Rushdi Saleh et al. introduced the OCA corpus of movie reviews and evaluated NB and SVM classifiers \cite{behdenna2018document}. 

\item In the Chinese domain, NB was found to outperform SVM for mobile review classification \cite{behdenna2018document}. 

\item Feature reduction techniques such as Principal Component Analysis (PCA) have been shown to improve both SVM and NB performance \cite{behdenna2018document}. 

\item Hybrid approaches combining NB with genetic algorithms and rating-based features augmented with n-grams have also demonstrated performance gains \cite{behdenna2018document}. 

\item For dialectal Arabic tweets, replacing dialectal words with Modern Standard Arabic equivalents improved classifier accuracy by up to 3\% \cite{behdenna2018document}.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Coarse-Grained versus Fine-Grained Supervised Learning}

A critical distinction exists between supervised learning applied at coarse granularities (document and sentence level) versus fine-grained aspect-based analysis \cite{azzeddine2018sentiment}. 

\begin{itemize}
	\item Features suitable for document and sentence level analysis are typically target-independent and thus inapplicable to aspect-level tasks, where the core objective is identifying opinion targets \cite{azzeddine2018sentiment}.
	 
	\item Aspect-level supervised learning therefore requires either target-dependent feature generation or mechanisms to determine the application scope of sentiments that cover specific entities or aspects within a sentence \cite{azzeddine2018sentiment}.
	
	\item Additionally, aspect-level supervised learning demands annotated datasets containing labeled aspects and non-aspects, which are more costly to produce than document-level polarity labels \cite{azzeddine2018sentiment}.
\end{itemize}

\section{Deep Learning for Sentiment Analysis}

Deep learning has revolutionized sentiment analysis by offering several advantages over traditional machine learning approaches \cite{sharma2025review}. Unlike methods that rely on manually crafted features, deep learning models can automatically discover and learn relevant representations directly from the data, eliminating time-consuming and potentially error-prone feature engineering \cite{sharma2025review}. They have the ability to adapt to new data, it leads to more robust and generalizable models \cite{sharma2025review}. This automatic feature learning capability is particularly beneficial for large-scale sentiment analysis tasks involving social media posts or customer reviews, where deep learning algorithms excel at processing vast amounts of data due to their parallel processing capabilities \cite{sharma2025review}.

Studies have consistently shown that deep learning models achieve superior accuracy compared to traditional methods such as SVM and Naïve Bayes \cite{behdenna2018document, sharma2025review}. Furthermore, deep learning offers versatility across various sentiment analysis tasks, from basic polarity classification to aspect-based analysis and emotion detection \cite{sharma2025review}.

\subsection{Word Embeddings}

A fundamental shift in neural natural language processing came with the introduction of word embeddings \cite{desantis2023conceptual}. Unlike sparse bag-of-words representations, embeddings map words to dense continuous vectors that capture semantic similarity \cite{desantis2023conceptual}. Two widely used embedding models are \textsc{Word2Vec} and \textsc{GloVe} \cite{pennington2014glove, desantis2023conceptual}. These pre-trained representations allow models to use semantic knowledge learned from massive corpora, providing a richer input representation than traditional feature engineering \cite{desantis2023conceptual, sharma2025review, behdenna2018document}.

\subsection{Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs)}

Convolutional neural networks, originally developed for computer vision, were successfully adapted to natural language processing tasks by Collobert and Weston in 2008 \cite{sharma2025review}. A CNN architecture for text typically consists of three main layers: an input layer that receives tokenized text, a feature extraction layer comprising convolutional and pooling operations, and a classification layer with fully connected networks \cite{sharma2025review}.

For sentiment analysis, CNNs excel at learning contextual features through filters that capture local patterns such as key phrases and n-gram indicators of sentiment \cite{sharma2025review}. One advantage of CNNs is their efficiency: compared to fully connected networks, they have fewer parameters, leading to faster training times \cite{sharma2025review}. Paredes-Valverde et al. proposed a deep learning approach combining pre-processing, word embeddings, and a CNN model, demonstrating that CNN outperformed traditional SVM and Naïve Bayes classifiers \cite{behdenna2018document}.

However, CNNs have limitations when dealing with long-term dependencies in text data, often requiring deep architectures that become computationally expensive \cite{sharma2025review}.

\subsection{Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs)}

Recurrent neural networks depart from feed-forward architectures by incorporating a memory element that retains information from previous computations \cite{sharma2025review}. This makes them naturally suited for sequential data such as text, as they can model sequences of varying lengths and capture long-range dependencies \cite{sharma2025review}.

However, standard RNNs suffer from vanishing and exploding gradient problems when processing long sequences, which hinders their ability to learn effectively from extended dependencies \cite{sharma2025review}. Despite these limitations, RNNs have demonstrated exceptional performance in sentiment analysis tasks, with experimental results continuing to support their effectiveness \cite{sharma2025review}.

\subsection{Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) and Gated Recurrent Unit (GRU)}

To address the vanishing gradient problem, Hochreiter and Schmidhuber (1997) proposed the long short-term memory (LSTM) network \cite{sharma2025review}. LSTMs incorporate a gating mechanism that allows memory cells to store information for longer durations and retrieve past computational results as needed \cite{sharma2025review}. The architecture employs three gates: an input gate controlling what information is stored, a forget gate determining what to discard, and an output gate regulating information passed to the next step \cite{sharma2025review}. Bidirectional LSTMs (BiLSTMs) process information in both directions, which is particularly valuable for identifying aspects and entities contributing to overall sentiment \cite{sharma2025review}.

The gated recurrent unit (GRU) offers a simpler alternative with only two gates: a reset gate that combines new input with previous information, and an update gate that controls retention and discarding \cite{sharma2025review}. GRUs provide computational efficiency comparable to LSTMs while maintaining similar effectiveness, with bidirectional variants (Bi-GRUs) also available \cite{sharma2025review}.

Tang et al. introduced neural network models combining convolutional and recurrent architectures for document-level sentiment classification \cite{behdenna2018document}. Their approach first learns sentence representations using either CNN or LSTM, then encodes the semantics and relations using gated RNNs \cite{behdenna2018document}. Experiments on IMDB and Yelp datasets showed that LSTM outperforms multi-filtered CNN for sentence representation \cite{behdenna2018document}.

\subsection{Transformer Architecture and BERT}

The most significant breakthrough in deep learning for NLP came with the Transformer architecture and its pre-trained variants \cite{vaswani2017attention}. BERT (Bidirectional Encoder Representations from Transformers), developed by Google in 2018, revolutionized language understanding through its bidirectional architecture that considers both preceding and following words in context \cite{sharma2025review}.

BERT leverages a self-attention mechanism to capture relationships between words within a sentence \cite{sharma2025review}. It follows a two-step approach: pre-training on massive text corpora using masked language modeling and next-sentence prediction, followed by fine-tuning on downstream tasks such as sentiment analysis \cite{sharma2025review}. BERT can tokenize text into subword units, handling various linguistic complexities, and has set new benchmarks across numerous NLP domains \cite{sharma2025review}.

For sentiment analysis, fine-tuned BERT models achieve state-of-the-art results \cite{joshy2022analyzing}. DistilBERT, a distilled version of BERT that is smaller, faster, and retains most of BERT's language understanding capabilities, has been shown to perform well on sentiment analysis tasks \cite{joshy2022analyzing}.

\subsection{Emerging Architectures: LLMs and GNNs}

\begin{itemize}
	\item \textbf{Large Language Models (LLMs)} are trained on massive and diverse text corpora, enabling them to understand complex language patterns, context, and even cultural references crucial for accurate sentiment analysis \cite{sharma2025review}. LLMs excel at tasks like sarcasm detection, where literal meaning differs from underlying sentiment, and can be fine-tuned for specific domains with minimal resources \cite{sharma2025review}.
	
	\item \textbf{Graph Neural Networks (GNNs)} represent another emerging direction, designed to work with data represented as graphs where nodes represent entities (such as words) and edges represent relationships \cite{sharma2025review}. GNNs capture complex interdependencies between words, making them particularly valuable for aspect-based sentiment analysis where sentiment toward different aspects must be identified \cite{sharma2025review}. They can also integrate external knowledge bases such as sentiment lexicons and word ontologies, achieving more nuanced understanding \cite{sharma2025review}.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Strengths and Limitations}

Deep learning offers powerful capabilities for sentiment analysis, achieving state-of-the-art performance across multiple tasks. However, these gains come with important trade-offs. A few statements can be made:

\begin{itemize}
	\item \textbf{Key strengths} include automatic feature learning, superior accuracy on large datasets, and versatility across tasks from basic polarity classification to aspect-based analysis \cite{sharma2025review}. Models can capture intricate relationships and adapt to new data, producing robust and generalizable systems \cite{sharma2025review}.
	
	\item \textbf{However}, these advantages come with substantial computational costs. Deep learning models typically require GPUs for training and inference, have large memory footprints, and are slower than classic models, making them less suitable for real-time applications and deployment on resource-constrained devices \cite{sharma2025review}.
	
	\item \textbf{Additionally}, optimal algorithm choice depends on the specific application and dataset: RNN variants (LSTM, GRU) excel with long-term dependencies, while CNNs offer efficiency for local pattern detection \cite{sharma2025review}. Recent research suggests RNNs are surpassing CNNs for many sentiment analysis tasks, particularly when long-range dependencies are involved \cite{sharma2025review}.
\end{itemize}

Granular Computing (GrC) is an emerging paradigm that mimics human problem-solving by processing information at multiple levels of granularity \cite{[]}. An \textit{information granule} is a collection of objects drawn together by indistinguishability, similarity, or functionality. Granules can be organised hierarchically—coarse granules provide an overview, while finer granules reveal details. This hierarchical structure aligns with the way humans naturally perceive and analyse complex phenomena.

Three fundamental concepts underpin GrC: \textit{granulation} (decomposing a whole into parts), \textit{organisation} (integrating parts into a whole), and \textit{causation} (associating causes and effects) \cite{[]}. In the context of text analysis, granules can be characters, words, phrases, sentences, or even higher-level concepts. Moving between these levels allows a system to balance abstraction and detail, potentially improving both efficiency and interpretability.

The hierarchical organization of sentiment analysis tasks aligns naturally with granular computing principles. \cite{missen2012review} reviewed opinion mining from word to document level in a detailed manner, highlighting the importance of social networks for opinion mining tasks. \cite{feldman2013techniques} presented a general architecture where the input corpus is converted to text, preprocessed, and then annotated by a Document Analysis Module---with annotation potentially attributed to whole documents, sentences, or fine-grained entities (aspects). This multi-level perspective establishes sentiment analysis as inherently amenable to granular approaches \cite{rachid2018sentiment}.


\subsection{Three-Way Decisions}

A closely related idea is the theory of three-way decisions \cite{[][]}, which extends the traditional binary classification (accept/reject) by introducing a third option: deferment or uncertainty. In many real-world scenarios, it is preferable to withhold a decision when evidence is insufficient. Three-way decisions have been successfully integrated with GrC to create models that can delay classification until more information becomes available, a concept particularly useful in dynamic environments.

\subsection{Prototype Theory and Conceptual Spaces}

In cognitive science, prototype theory \cite{[]} posits that natural categories are formed around prototypical examples. Conceptual spaces \cite{[]} provide a geometric framework where concepts correspond to convex regions, and prototypes are points within those regions. This aligns naturally with granular computing: a concept can be seen as a granule whose members are semantically similar words, and its centroid (cluster centre) serves as a prototype. Recent work has begun to bridge prototype theory and word embeddings \cite{[]}, demonstrating the potential of using clustering to derive concept-level representations for text.

\section{Granular Computing in Text Classification and Sentiment Analysis}

The literature contains numerous applications of GrC to text mining. Below we review the most relevant contributions, grouped by their focus.

\subsection{Data-Level Granularity}

A \textit{classified feature representation three-way decision model} was proposed by \cite{[]}. The method selects distinct feature subsets for positive and negative classes using fuzzy quotient space theory and variance-weighted mutual information. If a sample falls into an uncertain region, the full feature set is used. This approach speeds up classification and reduces noise, albeit at the cost of training three separate models.

Another data-level technique is presented in \cite{[]}, where granular computing is combined with deep learning for cyberbullying detection. The authors generate \textit{textual granules} via a “reverse pancake scramble” algorithm and \textit{numerical granules} from word embeddings, then augment the training set with these granules. The resulting model, GCDL, demonstrates improved robustness.

\subsection{Relational Granular Techniques}

One of the earliest GrC-based text classification methods \cite{[]} extracts two types of information from training data: semantic information (the degree to which a feature is associated with a class) and structural information (overlap between feature sets). For a new document, similarity is computed as a weighted combination of inclusion degree and association similarity, and the document is assigned to the class of the most similar decision granule. This relational approach captures both feature co-occurrence and class association.

\subsection{Bag-of-Concepts Approaches}

Moving beyond words, several authors have explored \textit{bag-of-concepts} (BoC) representations. \cite{[]} directly apply prototype theory: word embeddings are clustered into Voronoi regions, and each document is represented by a histogram of cluster memberships. The authors show that such concept-level representations, when combined with standard classifiers, can rival word-level methods while offering better interpretability.

Similarly, \cite{[]} extract m-grams, embed them using fastText, and cluster them per class to obtain class-specific alphabets of symbols. Documents are then converted into \textit{symbolic histograms} and classified with SVM. An evolutionary algorithm optimises hyperparameters and selects the most discriminative symbols.

\cite{[]} address the problem of unbalanced datasets by first extracting n-grams, then running a granular algorithm (RL-GRADIS) per class to obtain representative symbols (cluster centres). The union of these symbols forms an alphabet, and documents are converted into histograms. Again, SVM with feature selection yields the final classification.

\subsection{Retrainable and Dynamic Models}

In dynamic environments where data streams arrive over time, static models become outdated. \cite{[]} propose a temporal-spatial three-way granular computing framework for sentiment classification. Initially, a small training set is used to classify only confident samples into positive or negative; uncertain samples remain in a boundary region. As new data arrives, the model is updated and the boundary samples are re-evaluated. This iterative refinement gradually improves accuracy without retraining on the entire dataset.

Open-topic classification, where new topics may appear after deployment, is tackled by \cite{[]}. The model uses BERT embeddings and DBSCAN to discover unknown topics, then incrementally learns new classes. A three-way mechanism defers uncertain samples, allowing them to be revisited when more knowledge is accumulated.

\subsection{Targeted Sentiment Analysis}

Targeted (or topic-aware, aspect-based, or sometimes fine-grained) sentiment analysis aims to determine the sentiment expressed towards a specific aspect of a text. \cite{[]} devise a multi-level interactive attention network where granules at different levels (word, phrase, sentence) interact through attention mechanisms. The architecture computes influence matrices between child and parent granules, enabling fine-grained sentiment prediction.

At the document level, \cite{[]} propose a multi-granular joint sentiment-topic model (MgJST) that introduces a sentence layer between documents and words, and a sentiment layer between documents and topics. By modelling topics at the sentence level, the model captures nuanced sentiment–topic relationships, which is valuable for applications such as brand monitoring.

\subsection{Multi-Level and Interpretable Methods}

Interpretability is a key motivation for granular computing. \cite{[]} develop a fuzzy rule-based system for sentiment analysis using a divide-and-conquer strategy. Information granules at different levels allow local features to be extracted, reducing both instance complexity and fuzziness, and resulting in a more interpretable model.

\cite{[]} provide a broader perspective on GrC for text mining, emphasising that text can be represented at character, word, phrase, and concept levels. Integrating these multiple views can capture richer information and improve mining performance.

\section{Gap Analysis and Contribution}

Several key patterns emerge from the literature. Traditional machine learning models are computationally efficient but operate on a fixed vocabulary, making them sensitive to changes in wording. Deep learning models achieve high accuracy but require substantial computational resources, making them impractical for large-scale deployment. Granular computing offers a theoretical middle path by representing text at the conceptual level rather than the lexical level.

This thesis empirically investigates this middle path by proposing a framework that combines concept-level text representation with a three-way cascade classifier. Based on the experimental results, the contributions and limitations of this approach are summarized below.

\subsection*{Pros}
\begin{itemize}
	\item \textbf{Semantic generalization to unseen words:} By clustering Sentence-BERT embeddings into conceptual groups, the system can map out-of-vocabulary words to semantically similar prototypes. This allows the model to maintain performance when encountering new terminology, addressing a key limitation of traditional bag-of-words approaches.
	
	\item \textbf{Efficient allocation of computational resources:} The cascade architecture implements three-way decision theory by routing samples based on classification confidence. A lightweight model handles clear-cut cases, while only the most ambiguous samples are passed to the computationally intensive specialist model. This balances accuracy against computational cost.
	
    \item \textbf{Targeted improvement on difficult cases:} Fine-tuning a deep learning model exclusively on the uncertain samples identified by the cascade resulted in approximately 0.1\% accuracy improvement on the training set compared to a standard DistilBERT baseline. This confirms that the cascade can be further specialized through transfer-learning.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Cons}
\begin{itemize}
	\item \textbf{Loss of lexical detail through abstraction:} The most significant finding is a measurable trade-off inherent in dimensionality reduction. Mapping thousands of specific n-grams onto a smaller set of conceptual clusters inevitably loses fine-grained lexical information. This loss resulted in approximately 2\% lower accuracy for the standalone concept-based classifiers compared to their vocabulary-based counterparts. For instance, the baseline SVM achieved 91.48\% accuracy, while the concept-clustered SVM reached 89.12\%.
	
	\item \textbf{Upfront computational cost:} Generating deep embeddings and clustering them into conceptual groups requires significant preprocessing time and resources. Sentence-BERT was chosen specifically because it can embed multi-word phrases, unlike static models such as Word2Vec which are limited to single tokens. However, the results indicate that even these context-aware embeddings cannot fully preserve the lexical precision lost during clustering.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Verdict}
The two experiments point in opposite directions. Concept-level clustering failed to match the accuracy of vocabulary-based methods, showing that granular abstraction comes at a measurable cost. 

However, the cascade classifier succeeded in distributing workload efficiently, proving that three-way decisions can reduce computational expense while maintaining accuracy.
