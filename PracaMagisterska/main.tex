\section{Sentiment Analysis}

Sentiment analysis (or opinion mining) is an umbrella term for the computational study of people's opinions, sentiments, emotions, and attitudes expressed in written language \cite{Liu2012sentiment}. The term includes field names and slightly different tasks associated with them: sentiment analysis, opinion mining, opinion extraction, sentiment mining, subjectivity analysis, affect analysis, emotion analysis, review mining, etc. \cite{Liu2012sentiment} However, the main terms in academia remain both: sentiment analysis and opinion mining \cite{Liu2012sentiment}.

A foundational formalization was proposed by \cite{Liu2012sentiment}, who recognized that opinions are not monolithic but rather composed of multiple interacting elements. This insight led to defining an opinion as a quintuple:
\begin{equation}
	(e_i, a_{ij}, s_{ijkl}, h_k, t_l),
\end{equation}
where:
\begin{itemize}
	\item $e_i$ is the name of an $i$-th entity (e.g., a product, service, organization, or individual),
	\item $a_{ij}$ is an $j$-th aspect of $e_i$ (a specific feature or attribute of that entity),
	\item $h_k$ is the $k$-th opinion holder (the person or organization expressing the opinion),
	\item $t_l$ is the $l$-th instance when the opinion is expressed, and
	\item $s_{ijkl}$ is the sentiment expressed toward aspect $a_{ij}$ of entity $e_i$ at $t_l$ by $h_k$.
\end{itemize}

Following this definition, the main objective of sentiment analysis is to determine all quintuples present in given texts \cite{azzeddine2018sentiment}.

\subsection{Sentiment Analysis Levels}

Sentiment analysis tasks are typically categorized by their granularity of analysis, reflecting different levels at which opinions can be extracted and aggregated \cite{azzeddine2018sentiment}. The choice of granularity determines both the complexity of the task and the type of insights that can be obtained. Three main levels of analysis are recognized in the literature:

\begin{itemize}	
	\item \textbf{Document-level sentiment analysis (DLSA)} operates at the coarsest granularity, aiming to determine the overall opinion expressed in an entire document \cite{behdenna2018document}. Following the opinion quintuple formulation, DLSA identifies the overall sentiment of the opinion holder about the entity \cite{azzeddine2018sentiment}. Documents are typically classified as positive, negative, or neutral, under the assumption that the document focuses on a single entity or topic, and that the opinion is coherent \cite{sharma2025review}. The task commonly follows a two-step approach: Topic Relevance Retrieval followed by Opinion Finding \cite{azzeddine2018sentiment, missen2012opinion}. Considered the simplest sentiment analysis task, DLSA has been the most frequently studied granularity in the field, with 73 out of 159 reviewed articles operating at this level \cite{azzeddine2018sentiment, ravi2015survey}.
	
	\item \textbf{Sentence-level sentiment analysis (SLSA)} operates at a finer granularity by considering individual sentences as the unit of analysis \cite{behdenna2018document}. This level typically involves a two-step process. The first step, known as Subjectivity Classification, distinguishes subjective sentences (expressing opinions) from objective sentences (presenting facts) \cite{azzeddine2018sentiment, CHATURVEDI201865}. The second step, Sentence Sentiment Classification, then assigns polarity labels (positive, negative, or neutral) to subjective sentences \cite{azzeddine2018sentiment}. This approach is particularly useful when documents contain a mixture of factual statements and opinions \cite{sharma2025review}. However, a known limitation is that objective sentences can sometimes carry implicit sentiment \cite{behdenna2018document}. Furthermore, sentence-level analysis commonly assumes that each sentence expresses a single sentiment; sentences containing multiple opposing sentiments require more nuanced treatment \cite{azzeddine2018sentiment}.
	
	\item \textbf{Aspect-based sentiment analysis (ABSA)} represents the finest granularity, focusing on identifying sentiments expressed towards specific aspects or attributes of an entity \cite{yadav2020sentiment}. Rather than assigning a single polarity to an entire document or sentence, ABSA extracts both the target of opinion and its associated sentiment, corresponding to the first three components of the opinion quintuple: entity, aspect, and sentiment \cite{behdenna2018document, azzeddine2018sentiment}. For example, in a restaurant review, the sentiment expressed towards ``food'' may differ from that towards ``service'' \cite{sharma2025review}. This fine-grained analysis addresses the need to know exactly what people liked or disliked \cite{azzeddine2018sentiment}. Aspects may be expressed either explicitly or implicitly \cite{azzeddine2018sentiment}, and ABSA consequently requires two core subtasks: Aspect Extraction and Aspect-Level Sentiment Classification \cite{sharma2025review}.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Sentiment Analysis Tasks}

The explosive growth of user-generated content on social media, review platforms, and forums has made automated sentiment detection an essential tool for businesses, governments, and researchers \cite{sharma2025review}. Applications span brand monitoring, customer feedback analysis, political trend prediction, and mental health assessment \cite{sharma2025review}. More broadly, the field supports business intelligence, recommendation systems, review summarization, and government intelligence, demonstrating its impact \cite{behdenna2018document}. As recent comprehensive surveys discuss \cite{sharma2025review}, the field has evolved beyond simple polarity detection to address multiple complementary problems. Below are some of the recent fields of research:

\begin{itemize}	
	\item \textbf{Emotion detection (ED)} extends polarity classification by identifying fine-grained emotional states such as joy, anger, sadness, or frustration \cite{sharma2025review}. Unlike binary or ternary sentiment classification, emotion detection requires more expressive representations and often relies on specialized lexical resources or machine learning algorithms \cite{sharma2025review}.
	
	\item \textbf{Multi-domain sentiment classification} addresses the challenge of transferring models across different application domains \cite{sharma2025review}. For instance, a model trained on movie reviews may perform poorly on product reviews due to differences in vocabulary and linguistic patterns. Domain adaptation and transfer learning techniques are commonly employed to bridge this gap \cite{sharma2025review}.
	
	\item \textbf{Multilingual sentiment analysis} aims to analyse sentiment across multiple languages \cite{sharma2025review}. This task typically leverages language-specific resources such as sentiment lexicons, large translated corpora, or language detection systems as preprocessing steps \cite{sharma2025review}.
	
	\item \textbf{Multimodal sentiment analysis (MMSA)} integrates information from multiple channels, including text, audio, and visual signals \cite{sharma2025review}. By moving beyond purely textual analysis, MMSA enables more advanced applications such as analysing spoken reviews and vlogs, interpreting images and social media tags, and understanding human-machine interactions \cite{sharma2025review}.
	
	\item \textbf{Opinion spam detection (OSD)} focuses on identifying deceptive or fabricated opinions intended to mislead users or automated sentiment analysis tools \cite{sharma2025review}. These deceptive opinions include fake reviews designed to manipulate public perception and social media posts that artificially promote specific individuals, products, or services \cite{sharma2025review}.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Sentiment Analysis Challenges}

Despite its importance and wide-ranging applications, sentiment analysis faces several fundamental challenges that complicate accurate opinion mining. Opinion words are necessary but not sufficient for reliable sentiment detection, and multiple linguistic and contextual factors must be addressed \cite{behdenna2018document}.

\begin{itemize}	
	\item \textbf{Contextual Polarity Ambiguity} is the core difficulty: the same sentiment-bearing word can have different polarities depending on the context in which it appears \cite{behdenna2018document}. A word that is positive in one domain may be neutral or even negative in another.
	
	\item \textbf{Sarcasm and Irony Detection} presents particularly difficult challenges, as the literal meaning of an utterance contradicts its intended sentiment \cite{behdenna2018document, islam2024challenges}. When someone says something positive but actually means something negative, or vice versa, purely lexical approaches inevitably fail \cite{behdenna2018document}. Recent research has emphasized the need for improved models capable of classifying sarcasm and irony \cite{islam2024challenges}.
	
	\item \textbf{Negation Handling} is critical because the presence of negation words can reverse the polarity of an opinion \cite{behdenna2018document, islam2024challenges}.
	
	\item \textbf{Implicit and Objective Opinions} are not always straightforward. Many sentences containing opinion words may not actually express any opinion, while conversely, many sentences without explicit opinion words can convey strong sentiments \cite{behdenna2018document}. These are often objective sentences that present factual information yet carry implicit evaluative meaning \cite{behdenna2018document}.
	
	\item \textbf{Vocabulary Challenges} include rapid vocabulary evolution, including new words, slang, and abbreviations that constantly emerge-particularly on social media \cite{sharma2025review}. Any practical system must therefore be robust to such linguistic shifts \cite{sharma2025review}. Broader challenges identified in the literature include algorithm optimization, language model limitations, subjectivity detection, tone-setting polarities, and data inconsistency \cite{sharma2025review}. Multilingual analysis adds further complexity, requiring either language-specific resources or large translated corpora \cite{sharma2025review}.
	
\end{itemize}

\section{Sentiment Analysis Methods}

Sentiment analysis methods can be broadly divided into two main categories: traditional approaches and machine learning approaches \cite{sharma2025review}. Within machine learning, a further distinction exists between classic supervised approaches and modern deep learning techniques \cite{behdenna2018document}. A detailed explanation of each method can be found in the following sections.

\subsection{Lexicon-Based Methods}

Lexicon-based approaches represent a fundamental alternative to supervised machine learning, operating without requiring labelled training data \cite{azzeddine2018sentiment}. These methods rely on pre-compiled dictionaries of words annotated with sentiment scores (e.g., positive, negative, neutral) and are often considered a form of unsupervised sentiment analysis \cite{azzeddine2018sentiment}. The core assumption is that the collective polarity of a sentence or document can be derived from the polarities of its individual words and phrases \cite{azzeddine2018sentiment}.

\subsubsection{Dictionary-Based versus Corpus-Based Approaches}

Lexicon-based methods are divided into two main categories: dictionary-based and corpus-based approaches \cite{ravi2015survey,azzeddine2018sentiment, nursal2025battle}. 

\begin{itemize}	
\item \textbf{Dictionary-based approaches} utilise existing lexical databases containing opinion words along with their sentiment strengths \cite{azzeddine2018sentiment}. Popular sentiment lexicons in this category include SentiWordNet \cite{sentiwordnet2010}, WordNet-Affect \cite{strapparava2004wordnet}, and VADER \cite{hutto2015vader} as stated in \cite{nursal2025battle}. The dictionary-based method is generally more practical as it does not require a comprehensive corpus covering all possible words \cite{nursal2025battle}.

\item \textbf{Corpus-based approaches}, by contrast, rely on the probability of sentiment words co-occurring with positive or negative seed words within large text collections \cite{azzeddine2018sentiment}. This method is particularly useful for discovering new sentiment words from domain-specific corpora or for creating entirely new sentiment lexicons \cite{nursal2025battle}. However, its effectiveness depends on the availability of a sufficiently large and representative corpus \cite{nursal2025battle}.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Semantic Orientation and Pointwise Mutual Information}
A pioneering unsupervised algorithm was proposed by Turney \cite{azzeddine2018sentiment, behdenna2018document}, who introduced a method based on semantic orientation (SO). The algorithm proceeds in three steps: first, extracting two consecutive words whose part-of-speech tags follow a specific patterns; second, estimating the polarity of adjectives and adverbs using pointwise mutual information (PMI); and third, aggregating individual polarities to classify the entire review \cite{azzeddine2018sentiment}.

PMI measures the statistical dependence between a phrase and reference words (typically ``excellent'' and ``poor'') based on their co-occurrence in a corpus or across the web \cite{azzeddine2018sentiment}. A sentence receives positive SO when it has stronger associations with ``excellent'' and negative SO when associated with ``poor'' \cite{behdenna2018document}. Rothfels and Tibshirani extended this approach by using two seed reference sets rather than single words, improving domain adaptability  \cite{behdenna2018document}.

\subsubsection{Sentiment Lexicons}

Tools such as WordNet-Affect and SentiWordNet provide foundational lexical resources for sentiment analysis \cite{nursal2025battle}.

\begin{itemize}	
\item \textbf{WordNet} organises nouns, verbs, adjectives, and adverbs into synsets (sets of cognitive synonyms), each representing a distinct concept \cite{nursal2025battle}. WordNet 3.0 contains over 117,000 synsets, with semantic relations connecting them \cite{nursal2025battle}. WordNet is an example of a well-known semantic network where definitional networks focus on IsA relationships between a concept and a newly defined sub-type, supporting the rule of inheritance for copying properties defined for a super-type to all its sub-types \cite{CHATURVEDI201865}.

\item \textbf{SentiWordNet} builds upon WordNet by assigning three scores to each synset: positivity (P), negativity (N), and objectivity (O), which sum to 1 \cite{nursal2025battle}. Version 3.0 covers 147,306 synsets and has been widely adopted due to its extensive coverage of opinion words \cite{nursal2025battle}. However, SentiWordNet is sensitive to noise in datasets, particularly from social media, and does not account for internet slang or emoticons \cite{nursal2025battle}. Lexicons generated with automatic methods often include neutral words, introducing noise in the detection of subjectivity \cite{CHATURVEDI201865}.

\item \textbf{VADER} (Valence Aware Dictionary and sEntiment Reasoner) \cite{hutto2015vader} is an effective rule-based tool, which combines a sentiment lexicon with grammatical and syntactical cues such as intensifiers, negations, and punctuation \cite{nursal2025battle}. VADER was specifically developed for social media contexts and incorporates acronyms (e.g., ``LOL''), slang, and emojis \cite{nursal2025battle}. The dictionary comprises approximately 7,500 features, drawing from existing lexicons including LIWC, ANEW, and the General Inquirer \cite{nursal2025battle}. VADER offers several advantages: it works effectively with social media text, requires no training data, supports emoji sentiment classification, and achieves high processing speed \cite{nursal2025battle}.

\item \textbf{TextBlob} provides another accessible lexicon-based tool, offering simple polarity scoring with preprocessing capabilities through the Natural Language Toolkit (NLTK) \cite{nursal2025battle}.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Empirical Comparisons}

Comparative studies have evaluated these lexicons across various domains. Nursal et al. \cite{nursal2025battle} compared WordNet, SentiWordNet, TextBlob, and VADER on Malaysian high-rise property reviews, using human-annotated data as a baseline. Their findings revealed that all four lexicons identified a higher proportion of positive reviews than negative or neutral ones. WordNet recorded the highest number of positive sentiments, followed closely by SentiWordNet and VADER \cite{nursal2025battle}. For negative sentiment detection, VADER proved most effective, while for neutral sentiment, VADER again detected the highest number of reviews \cite{nursal2025battle}.

In terms of classification accuracy against human annotation, VADER achieved the best performance with 78.2\% accuracy, 74.3\% precision, 90.9\% recall, and an F1-score of 81.7\% \cite{nursal2025battle}. TextBlob ranked second, followed by SentiWordNet and WordNet with comparable performance around 69-70\% accuracy \cite{nursal2025battle}.

However, misclassifications occurred due to several factors: limited vocabulary coverage, difficulty detecting sarcasm, word ambiguity, and challenges with misspelled or abbreviated terms \cite{nursal2025battle}.

\subsubsection{Handling Sentiment Shifters and Composition}

Effective lexicon-based systems must account for sentiment shifters-linguistic elements that alter the polarity of words \cite{azzeddine2018sentiment, behdenna2018document}. Negations (e.g., ``not good'') reverse semantic polarity, while intensifiers (e.g., ``very good'') modify the degree of sentiment \cite{azzeddine2018sentiment}. Sophisticated approaches incorporate three pillars: a sentiment lexicon containing words, phrases, idioms, and composition rules; a set of rules for handling shifters and contrastive clauses (e.g., ``but''); and sentiment aggregation functions derived from parse trees \cite{azzeddine2018sentiment}.

\subsubsection{Preprocessing Considerations}

Preprocessing requirements vary across lexicons. While VADER can handle capitalization, punctuation, and colloquial expressions with minimal preprocessing, tools such as TextBlob, SentiWordNet, and WordNet require rigorous cleaning \cite{nursal2025battle}. Typical preprocessing steps include tokenisation, lowercase conversion, spell-checking, stemming, stopword removal, part-of-speech tagging, lemmatisation, and noise removal \cite{nursal2025battle}. Normalisation of abbreviations and acronyms is particularly important for social media text, as inconsistent vocabulary can degrade accuracy \cite{nursal2025battle}.

\subsubsection{Advantages and Limitations}

The main advantage of lexicon-based methods is that they require minimal effort for manual data annotation and do not need training datasets, making them readily applicable across domains \cite{nursal2025battle}. They are particularly competitive when labelled data is scarce \cite{azzeddine2018sentiment}. Domain dependency remains a challenge, as sentiment lexicons developed for one domain may perform poorly in another \cite{behdenna2018document}. Additionally, lexicon-based methods struggle with sarcasm, ambiguity, and the evolving nature of language, particularly on social media platforms \cite{nursal2025battle}.

\subsection{Classic Machine Learning Models}

With the availability of labelled datasets, supervised machine learning became the dominant paradigm for both coarse-grained and fine-grained sentiment analysis \cite{azzeddine2018sentiment}. Unlike lexicon-based methods, supervised approaches require annotated training data but can learn complex patterns from feature representations.

\subsubsection{Feature Engineering}

Generally text is first converted into a numerical representation, typically a bag-of-words (BoW) model where each document is represented by a vector of word frequencies. Variants include $n$-grams (contiguous sequences of words) and Term Frequency-Inverse Document Frequency (TF-IDF) weighting, which down-weights common words~\cite{behdenna2018document}.

Beyond these basic features, supervised approaches commonly employ syntactic features such as part-of-speech tags and dependency relations, as well as semantic features including sentiment words, phrases, and sentiment shifters \cite{azzeddine2018sentiment}. Feature engineering is the key to building effective sentiment analysis classifiers \cite{azzeddine2018sentiment}.

Sentence-level analysis is important because it permits a fine-grained view of different opinions expressed \cite{CHATURVEDI201865}. Sentence-level features can be in the form of $n$-grams, POS tags, or location based features \cite{CHATURVEDI201865}. Studies examining the different types of sentence features and their comparative effectiveness in determining sentiment analysis concluded that features such as length or rhetorical segments in text have little effect in accuracy and could sometimes decrease sentiment accuracy; similarly, $bi$-grams performed well without the need for POS features \cite{CHATURVEDI201865}.

\subsubsection{Classification Algorithms}

Classifiers such as Naïve Bayes (NB), Logistic Regression, and Support Vector Machines (SVM) are then trained on these feature vectors. SVM and NB, in particular, have been shown to be the most effective classifiers for sentiment analysis tasks \cite{behdenna2018document}. Extracted features are used to train state-of-the-art classifiers such as SVM and NB that assume that the class of a particular feature is independent of the class of other features given the training data \cite{CHATURVEDI201865}.

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Support Vector Machines (SVM)} perform well on high-dimensional sparse text data due to their margin-maximization objective and have been widely adopted for sentiment classification \cite{behdenna2018document}. 

\item \textbf{Naïve Bayes (NB)} offers simplicity and efficiency, often achieving competitive results despite its strong independence assumptions \cite{behdenna2018document}. 
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Empirical Findings}

\begin{itemize}
	\item Pang et al.~\cite{Pang2002thumbs} was the first to apply machine learning to movie review classification, showing that binary feature representation outperforms frequency-based representations.
	
	\item Pak and Paroubek \cite{Pak2011ClassificationEP} proposed sub-graph representations from syntactic dependency trees, showing that such structured features avoid information loss associated with pure $n$-gram models and improve SVM performance on French reviews.
	
	\item For Arabic sentiment analysis, Rushdi-Saleh et al.~\cite{Rushdi2011opinion} introduced the Opinion Corpus for Arabic (OCA) of movie reviews and evaluated NB and SVM classifiers, achieving promising results in comparison with Pang et al.~\cite{Pang2002thumbs}. It is noteworthy that the use of $n$-gram models overcomes the use of the unigram model~\cite{Rushdi2011opinion}.
	
	\item In the Chinese domain, NB was found to outperform SVM for mobile review classification \cite{Zhang2014sentiment}. 
	
	\item Feature reduction techniques such as Principal Component Analysis (PCA) have been shown to improve both SVM and NB performance \cite{Vinodhini2013effect}. 
	
	\item Hybrid approaches combining NB with genetic algorithms (GAs) and rating-based features augmented with $n$-grams have also demonstrated performance gains \cite{Govindarajan2014SentimentAO}. 
	
	\item For dialectal Arabic tweets, replacing dialectal words with Modern Standard Arabic equivalents improved classifier accuracy by up to 3\% \cite{Duwairi2015sentiment}.
\end{itemize}

The sources collectively indicate that appropriate data preprocessing and feature modeling directly impact classifier performance in sentiment analysis.

\subsubsection{Coarse-Grained versus Fine-Grained Supervised Learning}

A critical distinction exists between supervised learning applied at coarse granularities (document and sentence level) versus fine-grained aspect-based analysis \cite{azzeddine2018sentiment}. 

\begin{itemize}
	\item Features suitable for document and sentence level analysis are typically target-independent and thus inapplicable to aspect-level tasks, where the core objective is identifying opinion targets \cite{azzeddine2018sentiment}.
	 
	\item Aspect-level supervised learning therefore requires either target-dependent feature generation or mechanisms to determine the application scope of sentiments that cover specific entities or aspects within a sentence \cite{azzeddine2018sentiment}.
	
	\item Additionally, aspect-level supervised learning demands annotated datasets containing labeled aspects and non-aspects, which are more costly to produce than document-level polarity labels \cite{azzeddine2018sentiment}.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Deep Learning for Sentiment Analysis}

Deep learning has become very influential for sentiment analysis by offering several advantages over traditional machine learning approaches \cite{ain2017sentiment, sharma2025review}. Unlike methods that rely on manually crafted features, deep learning models learn implicitly from the training data, eliminating time-consuming and potentially error-prone feature engineering \cite{ain2017sentiment}. They have the ability to adapt to new data, it leads to more robust and generalizable models \cite{sharma2025review}. This automatic feature learning capability is particularly beneficial for large-scale sentiment analysis tasks involving social media posts or customer reviews, where deep learning algorithms excel at processing vast amounts of data due to their parallel processing capabilities \cite{sharma2025review}.

Studies have consistently shown that deep learning models achieve superior accuracy compared to traditional methods such as SVM and Naïve Bayes \cite{ain2017sentiment, behdenna2018document, sharma2025review}. Furthermore, deep learning offers versatility across various sentiment analysis tasks, from basic polarity classification to aspect-based analysis and emotion detection \cite{sharma2025review}.

These advantages come with substantial computational costs \cite{strubell2019energy}. Deep learning models typically require specialized hardware such as Graphics Processing Units (GPUs) or Tensor Processing Units (TPUs) for training, leading to significant financial expenses for hardware, electricity, or cloud compute time, as well as a considerable environmental impact due to carbon emissions \cite{strubell2019energy}. Deep learning models are data‑hungry and perform poorly when labeled data is scarce \cite{Li2017deep}. Another issue is the difficulty to interpret the predictions, as the models are often black boxes \cite{Li2017deep}.

\subsubsection{Word Embeddings}

A fundamental shift in neural Natural Language Processing (NLP) came with the introduction of word embeddings \cite{desantis2023conceptual}. Unlike sparse bag-of-words representations, embeddings map words to dense continuous vectors that capture semantic similarity \cite{desantis2023conceptual}. Two widely used embedding models are Word2Vec and GloVe \cite{pennington2014glove, desantis2023conceptual}. These pre-trained representations allow models to use semantic knowledge learned from massive corpora, providing a richer input representation than traditional feature engineering \cite{desantis2023conceptual, sharma2025review, behdenna2018document}.

\subsubsection{Convolutional Neural Networks}
Convolutional Neural Networks (CNN), originally developed for computer vision, were successfully adapted to NLP tasks by Collobert and Weston in 2008 \cite{Collobert2008unified}. The CNN architecture for text consists of three main layers: an input layer that receives tokenized text, a feature extraction layer comprising convolutional and pooling operations, and a classification layer with fully connected networks \cite{Collobert2008unified}.

For sentiment analysis, CNNs excel at learning contextual features through filters that capture local patterns such as key phrases and $n$-gram indicators of sentiment \cite{sharma2025review}. One advantage of CNNs is their efficiency: compared to fully connected networks, they have fewer parameters, leading to faster training times \cite{sharma2025review}. Paredes-Valverde et al. \cite{Paredes2017sentiment} proposed a deep learning approach combining pre-processing, word embeddings, and a CNN model, demonstrating that CNN outperformed traditional SVM and Naïve Bayes classifiers.

However, CNNs have limitations when dealing with long-term dependencies in text data, often requiring deep architectures that become computationally expensive \cite{sharma2025review}.


\subsubsection{Recurrent Neural Networks}
Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) depart from feed-forward architectures by incorporating a memory element that retains information from previous computations \cite{sharma2025review}. This makes them naturally suited for sequential data such as text, as they can model sequences of varying lengths and capture long-range dependencies \cite{sharma2025review}.

However, standard RNNs suffer from vanishing and exploding gradient problems when processing long sequences, which hinders their ability to learn effectively from extended dependencies \cite{sharma2025review}. Despite these limitations, RNNs have demonstrated exceptional performance in sentiment analysis tasks, with experimental results continuing to support their effectiveness \cite{sharma2025review}.

\subsubsection{Long Short-Term Memory and Gated Recurrent Unit}

To address the vanishing gradient problem, Hochreiter and Schmidhuber (1997) \cite{hochreiter1997long} proposed the long short-term memory (LSTM) network. LSTMs incorporate a gating mechanism that allows memory cells to store information for longer durations and retrieve past computational results as needed \cite{hochreiter1997long}. The architecture employs three gates: an input gate controlling what information is stored, a forget gate determining what to discard, and an output gate regulating information passed to the next step \cite{hochreiter1997long}. Bidirectional LSTMs (BiLSTMs) process information in both directions, which is particularly valuable for identifying aspects and entities contributing to overall sentiment \cite{sharma2025review}.

The gated recurrent unit (GRU) offers a simpler alternative with only two gates: a reset gate that combines new input with previous information, and an update gate that controls retention and discarding \cite{cho2014learning}. GRUs provide computational efficiency comparable to LSTMs while maintaining similar effectiveness, with bidirectional variants (Bi-GRUs) also available \cite{sharma2025review}.

Tang et al.~\cite{Tang2015document} introduced neural network models combining convolutional and recurrent architectures for DLSA. The framework first learns sentence representations using either CNN or LSTM, then encodes the semantics and relations using gated RNNs \cite{Tang2015document}. Experiments on Internet Movie Database (IMDB) and Yelp datasets show that LSTM outperforms multi-filtered CNN for sentence representation \cite{Tang2015document}.

\subsubsection{Transformer Architectures}

The most significant breakthrough in deep learning for NLP came with the transformer architecture and its pretrained variants \cite{vaswani2017attention}. Bidirectional Encoder Representations from Transformers (BERT), developed by Google in 2018 \cite{Delvin2018bert}, revolutionized language understanding through its bidirectional architecture that considers both preceding and following words in context \cite{Delvin2018bert}.

BERT leverages a self-attention mechanism to capture relationships between words within a sentence \cite{Delvin2018bert}. It follows a two-step approach: pretraining on massive text corpora using masked language modelling and next-sentence prediction, followed by fine-tuning on downstream tasks such as sentiment analysis \cite{Delvin2018bert}. BERT can tokenize text into subword units, handling various linguistic complexities, and has set new benchmarks across numerous NLP domains \cite{Delvin2018bert}.

For sentiment analysis, fine-tuned BERT models achieve state-of-the-art results \cite{joshy2022analyzing}. DistilBERT, a distilled version of BERT that is smaller, faster, and retains most of BERT's language understanding capabilities, has been shown to perform well on SA tasks \cite{joshy2022analyzing}.

\subsubsection{Emerging Architectures}

\begin{itemize}
	\item \textbf{Large Language Models (LLMs)} are trained on massive and diverse text corpora, enabling them to understand complex language patterns, context, and even cultural references crucial for accurate sentiment analysis \cite{sharma2025review}. LLMs excel at tasks like sarcasm detection, where literal meaning differs from underlying sentiment, and can be fine-tuned for specific domains with minimal resources \cite{sharma2025review}.
	
	\item \textbf{Graph Neural Networks (GNNs)} represent another emerging direction, designed to work with data represented as graphs where nodes represent entities (such as words) and edges represent relationships \cite{sharma2025review}. GNNs capture complex interdependencies between words, making them particularly valuable for aspect-based sentiment analysis where sentiment toward different aspects must be identified \cite{sharma2025review}. They can also integrate external knowledge bases such as sentiment lexicons and word ontologies, achieving more nuanced understanding \cite{sharma2025review}.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Architecture Choice}

The choice of the optimal architecture depends on the specific application and dataset. RNN variants (LSTM, GRU) excel at capturing long-term dependencies, whereas CNNs offer efficiency for local pattern detection \cite{sharma2025review}. Recent research indicates that RNNs outperform CNNs on many sentiment analysis tasks, particularly when long-range dependencies are involved \cite{sharma2025review}. Emerging architectures, such as LLMs and GNNs, have shown promising results for complex sentiment analysis problems \cite{sharma2025review}.

\section{Granular Computing}

Granular Computing (GrC) is a paradigm that processes complex information by treating data as aggregations known as information granules, the process of constructing them is known as Information Granulation (IG) \cite{bargiela2003granular}. The granules emerge from abstracting raw data into knowledge and appear in diverse forms: time intervals (hours, days, weeks), image regions where clusters of pixels form recognizable objects, and words in natural language \cite{bargiela2003granular}. The human cognitive capacity is limited, people naturally partition complex information into simpler blocks based on shared properties, GrC therefore exploits the human ability for problem-solving by operating at multiple levels of abstraction \cite{qui2006GrC}.

\subsection{Formalization and Core Principles}

An information granule is formally defined as a collection of elements grouped by indistinguishability, similarity, or functional proximity \cite{Liping2009GrC}. Granules can be arranged hierarchically: coarse granules provide a high-level overview, while finer granules reveal detailed information \cite{Liping2009GrC}.

\begin{description}
	\item Three fundamental concepts underpin GrC, as originally articulated by Zadeh \cite{Zadeh1997GrC}:
	\begin{itemize}
		\item \textbf{Granulation:} decomposition of the whole into parts,
		\item \textbf{Organization:} integration of parts into the whole,
		\item \textbf{Causation:} association of causes and effects.
	\end{itemize}
\end{description}  

Concrete mechanisms for granulation are often seen in computing and human reasoning \cite{bargiela2003granular}. In computer systems, memory resources are granulated by adopting memory pages as basic operational chunks; swapping techniques then enable efficient access to individual data items \cite{bargiela2003granular}. When describing a problem, people tend to avoid numbers, instead using aggregates and constructing if-then rules that operate on those aggregates \cite{bargiela2003granular}. Although the physical world is inherently analog, computers perform digital processing \cite{bargiela2003granular}. Furthermore, all data compression mechanisms represent instances of information granulation~\cite{bargiela2003granular}.

\subsection{Three-Way Decisions}

Three-way decision (3WD) is a theory of thinking, problem solving, and information processing that relies on the use of three parts, three elements, or three levels \cite{Yao2018threeway}. It extends the classical binary (two‑way) approach (typically acceptance or rejection) by introducing a third option: non‑commitment, deferment, or uncertainty \cite{Fujita2016threeway, Yao2018threeway}. In many real‑world situations, making an immediate decision is unwise when evidence is insufficient; 3WD provides a principled way to withhold judgment until more information can be obtained \cite{Yao2018threeway}.

The idea of trisecting a whole into three parts and then acting upon each part is a common human practice, evident in temporal (past-present-future), spatial (left-middle-right), volumetric (small-medium-large), and judgmental (acceptance-non‑commitment-rejection) divisions \cite{Fujita2016threeway}. Such a trisection can be formally represented by three pairwise disjoint subsets whose union is the universal set \cite{Fujita2016threeway}. Its cognitive basis lies in the limited capacity of human short‑term memory, which makes the number three a natural anchor-simple enough to process, yet richer than binary thinking \cite{Yao2018threeway}. Consequently, thinking in threes appears across many disciplines, from linguistics (three grammatical tenses) to computer science (three‑level database architectures) \cite{Yao2018threeway}.

\begin{description}
	\item A general model of 3WD is the Trisecting-Acting-Outcome (TAO) framework \cite{Yao2018threeway}:
	\begin{itemize}
		\item \textbf{Trisecting}: dividing a whole into three pairwise disjoint parts,
		\item \textbf{Acting}: devising strategies to process each of the three parts,
		\item \textbf{Outcome}: evaluating the effectiveness.
	\end{itemize}
\end{description}  

The connection between three‑way decision and granular computing is deep and natural \cite{Fujita2016threeway, Yao2018threeway}. Both paradigms are human‑inspired and rely on abstraction: granular computing builds information granules at multiple levels, while three‑way decision uses three granules (or three levels) as a particularly effective cognitive structure \cite{Yao2018threeway}. In fact, sequential three‑way decisions arise naturally from multiple levels of granularity \cite{Fujita2016threeway}. At a higher (coarser) level of granularity, one may defer decisions about some objects to a lower (finer) level where additional detailed information becomes available \cite{Fujita2016threeway}. This interplay has led to the integration of three‑way decision and granular computing into what is sometimes called three‑way granular computing \cite{Fujita2016threeway, Yao2018threeway}.

\subsection{Prototype Theory and Conceptual Spaces}

Prototype theory is another term from the field of cognitive sciences; it states that natural categories are organized around prototypical examples, where certain members of a category are judged to be more representative than others \cite{DeSantis2023prototype}. This theory emerged from experimental studies on human categorization and has become a foundational concept in cognitive psychology and linguistics \cite{DeSantis2023prototype}. 

Conceptual spaces provide a geometric framework in which concepts correspond to convex regions within a multidimensional space \cite{DeSantis2023prototype}. In such a space, each dimension represents a measurable quality or feature, and points within a region stand for individual instances of a concept \cite{DeSantis2023prototype}. The prototype of a category is then identified as a point, often the centroid, inside its corresponding region \cite{DeSantis2023prototype}. A key property is that natural properties form convex regions; meaning that if two points belong to the same concept, all points between them also belong to that concept \cite{DeSantis2023prototype}.

This geometric view aligns naturally with granular computing \cite{DeSantis2023prototype}. A concept can be seen as an information granule whose members are semantically similar objects, and the prototype serves as the granule's central representative \cite{DeSantis2023prototype}. The Voronoi tessellation of a conceptual space, obtained by partitioning the space according to the closest prototype, provides a constructive way to generate such granules \cite{DeSantis2023prototype}. Thus, conceptual spaces offer a cognitive grounding for granular computing, bridging the subsymbolic level of distributed representations (e.g., word embeddings) and the symbolic level of discrete concepts~\cite{DeSantis2023prototype}.

\subsection{Granular Computing for Text Classification}

Granular Computing has been applied to a wide range of text classification tasks, including topic categorization, spam detection, sentiment analysis, and hate speech identification~\cite{Liu2017fuzzy, Yang2022temporal, Behzadidoost2024GrC}. The core idea is to transform raw textual data into information granules at multiple levels of abstraction, thereby reducing dimensionality, improving interpretability, or enabling advanced decision-making. The most relevant contributions are reviewed below, grouped by their focus.

\subsubsection{Relational Methods}

Qiu et al.~\cite{qui2006GrC} propose an early granular computing framework for text classification that constructs a granule library containing feature granules (keywords with class sets), effective decision granules (feature subsets that uniquely determine a class), and association degrees between features and classes. A new document is converted into an information granule by combining its feature granules, then matched against decision granules via weighted similarity for classification~\cite{qui2006GrC}. The authors note that the method has not been tested in practical applications and requires further investigation of similarity computation and inclusion degrees~\cite{qui2006GrC}.

\subsubsection{Data-Level Methods}

Chen et al.~\cite{Chen2022threeway} propose a three-way decision model for binary sentiment analysis that selects distinct feature subsets for the positive and negative classes, rather than using a uniform representation. During testing, confident samples are classified using their class-specific optimal feature subset, while uncertain samples fall into a boundary region and are processed with the full feature set~\cite{Chen2022threeway}. Experiments on IMDB and Amazon show improved accuracy over chi-square and PCA-based feature selection, albeit with increased computational overhead~\cite{Chen2022threeway}.

Behzadidoost et al.~\cite{Behzadidoost2024GrC} propose a Granular Computing-based Deep Learning model (GCDL) for cyberbullying and hate speech detection. The model generates two types of granules: textual granules, constructed by a ``reverse pancake-scramble'' algorithm that flips characters and reverses vowels, and numerical granules, extracted via ten rule-based features (e.g., hashtags, stop words, emojis)~\cite{Behzadidoost2024GrC}. Textual granules are processed by a stacked CNN-LSTM, while numerical granules feed into a stacked BiLSTM-SVM – the first such combination in granular computing~\cite{Behzadidoost2024GrC}. An attention mechanism merges both outputs for final classification~\cite{Behzadidoost2024GrC}. On three datasets (cyberbullying and two hate speech collections), GCDL achieves 88.74\%, 89.62\%, and 70.85\% accuracy, outperforming state-of-the-art models~\cite{Behzadidoost2024GrC}. Ablation studies confirm that both granule types contribute positively, and the granulation process does not exceed the original dataset size, avoiding imbalance~\cite{Behzadidoost2024GrC}.

\subsubsection{Three-Way Decision Methods}

In dynamic environments where data streams arrive over time, static sentiment classifiers become outdated~\cite{Yang2022temporal}. Yang et al.~\cite{Yang2022temporal} propose a temporal-spatial three-way granular computing framework that incrementally updates the model as new data arrives~\cite{Yang2022temporal}. At each time step, the model classifies samples into three regions: positive, negative, and boundary. Confident samples are accepted or rejected immediately, while uncertain ones are deferred to the next level, where finer granularity (more data and updated parameters) enables more reliable decisions~\cite{Yang2022temporal}. This sequential three-way strategy reduces misclassification and time costs without sacrificing accuracy, as shown on IMDB, Yelp, and Amazon datasets using TextCNN, TextRNN, TextRCNN, and fastText~\cite{Yang2022temporal}.

For open topic classification where test data may contain unseen classes, the authors propose Three-Way Multi-Granularity learning towards Open topic classification (TWMG-Open)~\cite{Yang2022threeway}. The model operates at three granular levels: first, known topics are separated from unknowns using adaptive spherical decision boundaries; second, unknown samples are clustered via three-way Density-Based Spatial Clustering of Applications with Noise (DBSCAN) to discover hidden classes; third, high-confidence clusters are added to a knowledge base for continual learning~\cite{Yang2022threeway}. Experiments on Banking77, StackOverflow, and TC20 show that TWMG-Open outperforms traditional baselines, especially when the proportion of known classes is high~\cite{Yang2022threeway}.

\subsubsection{Bag-of-Concepts Methods}

Moving beyond word-level representations, several authors have explored bag-of-concepts (BoC) approaches that aggregate semantically related tokens into higher-level information granules~\cite{Capillo2020mining, DeSantis2023prototype, Possemato2013AutomaticTC}. De Santis et al.~\cite{DeSantis2023prototype} directly apply prototype theory: word embeddings are clustered into Voronoi regions, and each document is represented by a histogram of cluster memberships. This concept-level representation can rival word-level methods for text processing tasks, while offering better interpretability~\cite{DeSantis2023prototype}.

Capillo et al.~\cite{Capillo2020mining} present a granular computing framework that mines $m$-grams (short phrases) as information granules for text categorization. Each word in an $m$-gram is mapped to a vector using fastText embeddings, and the resulting sequence of vectors is compared using a custom edit distance that operates on word vectors rather than characters~\cite{Capillo2020mining}. A per-class clustering procedure extracts representative $m$-grams, which serve as alphabet symbols (concepts)~\cite{Capillo2020mining}. Documents are then transformed into symbolic histograms counting the occurrences of these concepts, and the histograms are classified by a SVM~\cite{Capillo2020mining}. A GA optimises the system’s hyperparameters and selects the most discriminative symbols~\cite{Capillo2020mining}. Experiments on scientific abstracts (four classes) achieve about 93\% accuracy, and on an SMS spam dataset about 95\% accuracy~\cite{Capillo2020mining}. Importantly, the selected symbols are interpretable phrases (e.g.,``string field theory'' for theoretical physics,``customer care'' for spam), demonstrating that the BoC approach yields both high performance and explainable models~\cite{Capillo2020mining}.

Possemato et al.~\cite{Possemato2013AutomaticTC} extend the BoC approach to handle unbalanced datasets by extracting $n$-grams and then running a per-class granular mining algorithm known as Reinforcement Learning Granular Approach for DIscrete
Sequence (RL-GRADIS) to obtain representative symbols for each category~\cite{Possemato2013AutomaticTC}. The union of these per-class symbols forms the final alphabet, and documents are converted into symbolic histograms by counting occurrences of each symbol~\cite{Possemato2013AutomaticTC}. A SVM with a Gaussian kernel then classifies the histograms, and a GA performs feature selection to retain only discriminative symbols~\cite{Possemato2013AutomaticTC}. Experiments on the unbalanced Reuters-21578 dataset (ten categories) show that the per-class strategy improves results compared to the standard unsupervised version, demonstrating better handling of sparse classes~\cite{Possemato2013AutomaticTC}. The extracted symbols remain interpretable~\cite{Possemato2013AutomaticTC}.

\subsubsection{Hierarchical Methods}

Liu and Cocea \cite{Liu2017fuzzy} propose a hierarchical, fuzzy rule-based system for sentiment analysis to address the high dimensionality inherent in traditional BoW models. Their approach decomposes documents into sub-levels (such as sections, paragraphs, and sentences) and subsequently groups these localized fragments into similar clusters \cite{Liu2017fuzzy}. The model extracts BoW features from ``small and simple'' cluster vocabularies, effectively bypassing the sparsity of a global matrix \cite{Liu2017fuzzy}. The system then applies fuzzy logic to score these local granules, aggregating the membership degrees upward to classify the whole text \cite{Liu2017fuzzy}. This method builds on their prior baseline experiments; while those early models achieved competitive accuracy, this hierarchical iteration successfully mitigates their large dimensionality while preserving the transparency of natural-language ``if-then'' rules~\cite{Liu2017fuzzy}.

Huang et al.~\cite{Huang2022multigranular} propose a Multi-granular Joint Sentiment-Topic model (MgJST) that extends sentiment topic models based on Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA) by introducing two additional layers: a sentence layer between documents and words, and a sentiment layer between documents and topics~\cite{Huang2022multigranular}. Unlike previous models such as Joint Sentiment Topic (JST), which treat a document as a single topic mixture, MgJST assigns a distinct topic distribution to each sentence within a document, capturing finer-grained structural knowledge~\cite{Huang2022multigranular}. The model is a five-layer hierarchical architecture that uses sampling for parameter estimation and can determine document-level sentiment polarity via a voting rule~\cite{Huang2022multigranular}. Experiments on seven review datasets show that MgJST significantly outperforms unsupervised baselines in sentiment detection and achieves competitive performance with supervised deep learning methods~\cite{Huang2022multigranular}. The extracted topics are interpretable, demonstrating the model's ability to reveal opinionated topics at multiple granularities~\cite{Huang2022multigranular}.

In a related multi-granular approach for targeted sentiment analysis, \cite{Li2020GrC} propose Granular Computing-based Multi-level Interactive Attention (GC-MIA) networks. Each word is treated as an indivisible granule, and a three-level interactive attention mechanism refines the representations of both the target and its context~\cite{Li2020GrC}. The first level uses matrix-based interactive attention to capture pairwise word interactions; the second level incorporates Euclidean distance to encode semantic closeness; the third level applies interactive attention between child granules (individual words) and parent granules (the aggregated target or context) to assign sentiment-relevant weights~\cite{Li2020GrC}. The final context and target granules are merged into an ancestor granule and fed to a softmax classifier~\cite{Li2020GrC}. On the SemEval 2014 laptop and restaurant datasets, GC-MIA achieves accuracies of 73.7\% and 79.0\%, respectively, outperforming LSTM, Time Distributed LSTM (TD-LSTM), and the original Interactive Attention Networks (IANs)~\cite{Li2020GrC}. A case study further visualises that the model assigns higher attention weights to context words semantically or sentimentally relevant to a given target, illustrating the explanatory power of the multi-level granular design~\cite{Li2020GrC}.

\subsection{Gap Analysis and Contribution}

\subsubsection{Gap Analysis}

The preceding sections have surveyed sentiment analysis at three levels (document, sentence, aspect) and highlighted persistent challenges: contextual polarity ambiguity, sarcasm and irony, negation handling, implicit opinions, and vocabulary evolution \cite{behdenna2018document, sharma2025review}. Existing methods fall into three broad families: lexicon-based (interpretable but fragile), classic machine learning (efficient but feature-sensitive), and deep learning (accurate but computationally expensive). Granular computing offers a conceptual bridge, but existing GrC work for text has not fully exploited the trade-offs within sentiment analysis.

\subsubsection{Gap 1 -- No efficient cascade that combines a lightweight classifier with a deep learning model for uncertain cases in sentiment analysis.} 
Sequential three-way decision frameworks (e.g., Yang et al.~\cite{Yang2022temporal}) defer uncertain samples to finer granularities, but they apply the same deep architecture at all levels, incurring high costs even for easy samples. In sentiment analysis, many reviews are clearly positive or negative and could be handled by a fast model (e.g., SVM), while only ambiguous or sarcastic cases require a large transformer like BERT. No existing method implements such a cascade with a principled deferral threshold for sentiment analysis.

\subsubsection{Gap 2 -- Bag-of-Concepts via n-gram mining has been validated for topic and spam classification but has not been evaluated for sentiment analysis.}
Capillo et al.~\cite{Capillo2020mining} and Possemato et al.~\cite{Possemato2013AutomaticTC} show that mining representative n-grams and clustering them into concept granules works well for categorising scientific abstracts and detecting spam. However, sentiment analysis differs: polarity often depends on negation, sarcasm, and subtle phrase-level cues. Whether the same pipeline can capture sentiment-specific phenomena -- and whether the resulting concept granules are interpretable for polarity detection -- remains an open question.
